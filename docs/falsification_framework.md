# Falsification Framework / 证伪框架

## Principle
Every investment thesis must be falsifiable. A thesis that cannot be proven wrong is a belief, not an investment thesis.

## 1. Core Thesis Falsification Table

| Thesis Statement | Falsification Indicator | Threshold | Data Source | Action if Triggered |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue growth sustainable | Growth rate decline | <5% for 2 quarters | Quarterly report | Downgrade to review |
| Gross margin stable | Margin compression | >3 ppt decline | Quarterly report | Reduce multiple |
| Market share gaining | Competitor wins key account | Loss of top-3 customer | Industry data | Rerun competition model |
| FCF improving | FCF margin declines | <7% | Cash flow statement | Reduce rating |
| Valuation reasonable | EPS/FCF revise down | >10% cut | Consensus revision | Cut target price |
| Capital allocation discipline | Debt/EBITDA increases | >3.0x | Balance sheet | Review leverage risk |
| Regulatory environment stable | New regulation proposed | Material impact >5% EPS | Regulatory filings | Reduce position size |
| ROIC trending higher | ROIC flat or declining | 2 consecutive periods | Annual report | Reassess competitive advantage |
| Pricing power intact | ASP decline without volume offset | >2% ASP decline | Segment reporting | Reduce margin assumptions |
| Customer retention high | Churn rate increases | >5% annual churn | Operating metrics | Rerun LTV model |

## 2. What the Market May Be Wrong About

**Markets overly optimistic about:**
- **Growth sustainability** — extrapolating recent trends without mean reversion
- **Margin expansion** — assuming operating leverage without fixed-cost visibility
- **Moat durability** — underestimating competitive response or technological disruption
- **Regulatory stability** — pricing regulatory risk as a tail event when it is structural
- **Management capital allocation** — giving credit for past deals without integration track record
- **TAM expansion** — assuming addressable market grows without execution proof
- **Multiple expansion** — paying up for duration in a rising-rate environment

**Our analysis overly pessimistic about:**
- **Competitive dynamics** — underestimating switching costs or network effects
- **Management execution** — assuming past failures repeat despite structural changes
- **New product cycles** — discounting pipeline value before proof of revenue
- **Market expansion** — ignoring optionality in adjacent verticals or geographies
- **Cyclical recovery** — treating cyclical troughs as permanent impairment
- **Self-help potential** — missing restructuring, refranchising, or portfolio optimization
- **Brand equity** — valuing brand only through current P&L without renewal visibility

## 3. Strongest Bull Case vs Strongest Bear Case

Must articulate both. If the bull case doesn't make you uncomfortable and the bear case doesn't sound plausible, you haven't analyzed enough.

| Dimension | Bull Case | Bear Case |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | New products + market share gains drive reacceleration | Core market saturates, share losses accelerate |
| Margins | Operating leverage from scale and automation | Inflation + price competition squeeze margins |
| Competitive Position | Moat widening via R&D and brand investment | Disruption from new entrants or technology shift |
| Capital Returns | FCF inflection funds buybacks and dividends | FCF consumed by rising capex and debt service |
| Valuation | Multiple expansion as quality premium recognized | Multiple compression as growth disappoints |
| M&A Optionality | Tuck-in acquisitions accelerate growth | Acquisitions destroy value and distract management |

### Bull Case Stress Test
- What needs to go perfectly for the bull case to materialize?
- What is the probability that each assumption fails?
- If the bull case does not materialize within 12 months, what is the downside?

### Bear Case Stress Test
- What catalyst could trigger the bear case?
- How quickly would the bear case play out?
- At what entry price does the bear case stop mattering (maximum pain priced in)?

## 4. Falsification Principles

1. **Every conclusion needs a measurable falsification condition.** If you cannot define the data point that would make you change your mind, you haven't formed a testable thesis.

2. **Risk warnings without thresholds are noise.** "Revenue may decline" is useless. "Revenue declining more than 5% for two quarters triggers thesis review" is actionable.

3. **If no data can change your mind, you're believing, not analyzing.** A thesis held in spite of all evidence is an ideology, not an investment.

4. **Update falsification framework when new information arrives.** The confirmation-bias trap is updating the thesis to fit the data instead of updating the framework to capture new risks. Treat every material data release as an opportunity to sharpen, not defend, your criteria.

5. **A tested thesis is stronger; an untested thesis is fragile.** A thesis that has survived multiple falsification attempts carries more conviction than one that has never been stress-tested. Seek out disconfirming evidence deliberately.

6. **Falsification thresholds must be set ex ante.** Defining thresholds after seeing the data introduces hindsight bias. Set them at thesis inception and change them only with documented rationale.

7. **Separate thesis falsification from position management.** A triggered falsification condition initiates review — it does not automatically liquidate. The review determines whether the thesis is broken or merely delayed.

## 5. Falsification Review Process

```
Trigger Event
    │
    ▼
Stage 1: Flag (same day)
    └─ Log the trigger, initial assessment (1-page memo)
    │
    ▼
Stage 2: Review (within 5 business days)
    └─ Full thesis re-evaluation against current data
    └─ Updated falsification table
    └─ Recommendation: Maintain / Adjust / Exit
    │
    ▼
Stage 3: Decision
    └─ Maintain: Document rationale for unchanged view
    └─ Adjust: Update target price, rating, position size
    └─ Exit: Execute liquidation within predetermined timeframe
    │
    ▼
Stage 4: Post-mortem (within 30 days of exit)
    └─ What was the falsification trigger?
    └─ Was the threshold appropriate?
    └─ What would have caught the failure earlier?
    └─ Lessons for future thesis construction
```

## 6. Common Falsification Failures

| Failure Mode | Description | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| **False precision** | Setting thresholds so tight they trigger on noise | Use trailing averages, not single-period data |
| **Threshold drift** | Quietly moving goalposts as evidence accumulates | Require documented rationale for any threshold change |
| **Recency bias** | Overweighting the latest data point | Require minimum 2-3 data points before triggering |
| **Narrative anchoring** | Interpreting disconfirming data through the original story | Assign a second analyst to independently assess the trigger |
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | Failing to exit because of time invested | Pre-commit to exit timeframe at Stage 1 |
| **False negatives** | Thresholds so wide nothing ever triggers | Review thresholds annually against realized outcomes |
