# Competition & Share Model / 竞争与份额模型

## 1. Market Boundary Definition

### TAM (Total Addressable Market)
The total revenue opportunity available if 100% market share were achieved across all segments. Defined as:

**TAM = Total Number of Potential Customers × Average Revenue per Customer**

| Dimension | Breakdown |
|---|---|
| **Region** | North America, Europe, APAC, ROW — segmented by regulatory climate, purchasing power, and adoption maturity |
| **Product** | Core product lines, adjacent offerings, platform / ecosystem revenue |
| **Customer** | Enterprise (500+ employees), Mid-market (50–499), SMB (1–49), Consumer |
| **Price Tier** | Premium, Standard, Budget / Freemium — each with distinct ARPU and volume characteristics |
| **Channel** | Direct sales, Partner / VAR, Online self-serve, OEM, Marketplace |
| **Application** | Primary use cases (e.g., CRM, data infrastructure, security, collaboration) — each with different willingness-to-pay and switching costs |

### SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)
The portion of TAM that the company's current products, geographies, and channel infrastructure can realistically reach within the planning horizon.

**SAM = TAM segments served by current product portfolio × percentage of geographies where the company has (or can build) a presence**

**SAM Filter Checklist:**
- Product-market fit verified in at least 2 reference markets
- Distribution channel exists or can be built within 12–18 months
- Regulatory compliance achievable at acceptable cost
- Localisation gap (language, cultural, workflow) is bridgeable

### SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)
The share of SAM the company can realistically capture given current competitive position, brand strength, balance sheet, and go-to-market maturity.

**SOM = SAM × Expected Market Share (based on current share, trajectory, and competitive dynamics)**

**SOM Constraints:**
- Sales capacity (quota-carrying headcount × ramp time)
- Marketing budget relative to category noise level
- Product readiness vs. competitive feature parity
- Reference-ability and analyst positioning

> **Key Question:** Is SAM growing faster or slower than TAM? If slower, the company is narrowing its addressable aperture — a conscious strategy choice or a warning sign.

## 2. Value Chain & Profit Pool

```
Supplier → Company → Channel → Customer → End User
```

### Profit Pool Mapping

| Node | Profit Margin | Who Captures Largest Profit? | Who Controls the Scarce Resource? | Who Controls Customer Access? | Who Bears Most Risk? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Supplier | Low / Commodity | Supplier parity in most mature categories | Supplier (raw materials, IP inputs) | None (sells to the company, not to customers) | Low — diversified across many buyers |
| Company | Medium / High (scale-dependent) | Platform / product companies with proprietary IP | Company (technology, brand, data) | Company owns the relationship when direct; shared when channel-mediated | Medium — R&D, inventory, market adoption risk |
| Channel | Low / Medium (aggregation margin) | Aggregators / distributors with network effects | Channel (last-mile access in fragmented markets) | Channel controls purchasing decision in channel-dominated verticals | Low — pass-through model, low capital intensity |
| Customer | N/A (cost center) | — | Customer controls budget and switching decision | Customer is the access point | Low (procurement risk, integration risk) |
| End User | N/A (consumption) | — | Attention / engagement | — | Minimal |

### Diagnostic Questions

| # | Question | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Who captures the largest profit in the value chain? | If not the company, the business model needs restructuring |
| 2 | Who controls the scarce resource? | Scarce resource holder dictates terms — is the company the resource owner? |
| 3 | Who controls the customer access point? | Gatekeeper power; channel disintermediation risk |
| 4 | Who bears the most risk? | Risk without commensurate return suggests a poor position in the value chain |
| 5 | Is the company at the center of the profit pool? | Central = difficult to bypass; peripheral = easily replaced |

### Value Chain Dynamics

- **Profit pool migration:** Is profit shifting upstream or downstream? (e.g., hardware → software → services)
- **Disintermediation risk:** Could a platform (e.g., cloud marketplace, Shopify) bypass the company's channel?
- **Vertical integration threat:** Are suppliers or customers forward/backward integrating?
- **Scarce resource trajectory:** Is the scarce resource becoming more or less scarce over time?

## 3. Moat Type

### Moat Typology

| Moat Type | Description | How Measured | Strengthening or Weakening? | Expected Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cost advantage** | Structural cost structure lower than competitors (scale, process, location) | Gross margin delta vs. #2/#3 competitor; unit cost curve | Weakening if rivals close the gap via automation or offshoring | 3–5 years (erodes with technology diffusion) |
| **Technology** | Proprietary IP, R&D pipeline, algorithmic advantage | Patent portfolio, R&D spend / revenue %, feature lead (months ahead) | Strengthening with compounding R&D; weakening on key patent cliffs | 5–10 years (depends on IP life and talent retention) |
| **Network effects** | Value increases as more users join the platform | Metcalfe's law proxy (users²); retention × referral rate | Strengthening with each marginal user; weakening if multi-homing becomes cheap | 10+ years (hardest to replicate) |
| **Brand** | Customer preference driven by trust, status, or habit | Unaided recall %, NPS, price premium vs. generic | Strengthening with consistent quality; weakening with scandals or category commoditisation | 10+ years (if maintained) |
| **Channel** | Exclusive or preferential access to distribution | Channel partner count, revenue per partner, partner churn | Strengthening with partner lock-in (training, integration); weakening if a new channel emerges | 5–7 years (channel disruption risk) |
| **Data** | Proprietary dataset that improves product (flywheel) | Data volume, recency, uniqueness; model accuracy improvement per data unit | Strengthening with use; weakening if data becomes commoditised or regulation limits collection | 5–10 years (regulatory and privacy headwinds) |
| **Regulatory license** | Permits, certifications, regulatory barriers to entry | Number of regulated markets entered; time/cost for a new entrant to get licensed | Stable or weakening (regulatory capture risk, deregulation trends) | Variable — can be permanent (utility) or temporary (fintech sandbox) |
| **Ecosystem lock-in** | High switching costs due to integrations, workflows, data portability | Switching cost $ (time + money + risk); API dependency count | Strengthening with deeper integration; weakening with interoperability standards | 7–10 years (open standards are a risk) |
| **Scale** | Fixed cost leverage; purchasing power | Revenue relative to #2 in market; cost of goods sold delta | Stable if market grows; weakening if growth slows and fixed costs spread thin | 3–7 years (depends on market growth rate) |
| **Capital advantage** | Access to cheaper capital (lower WACC, longer duration) | WACC vs. competitors; cash on hand; debt capacity | Strengthening in rising-rate environments (strong balance sheets); weakening in frothy markets | 2–5 years (capital cycles) |
| **Supply chain** | Proprietary or exclusive sourcing, vertical integration, geographic concentration | Supplier concentration; lead time advantage; input cost advantage | Strengthening with exclusive contracts; weakening if alternative supply emerges | 3–7 years (depends on raw material scarcity) |

### Moat Assessment Questions

1. **What type of moat(s) does the company have?** (List primary and secondary — most companies have 1–2 real moats)
2. **How is the moat measured quantitatively?** (Define the metric and current value)
3. **Is the moat strengthening or weakening?** (What is the 3-year trend?)
4. **How long will the moat last?** (Based on competitive response time, technology cycles, regulatory changes)
5. **Is the moat widening or narrowing relative to the #2 competitor?**
6. **Can the company reinvest moat returns into a second moat?** (e.g., brand → channel, technology → data)

## 4. Market Share Change Model

### Unified Formula

```
Company Revenue = Market Size × Industry Penetration × Company Market Share × Price / Take Rate × Product Mix
```

| Component | Definition | Measurement |
|---|---|---|
| **Market Size** | Total available revenue pool (TAM) | Analyst reports, bottom-up sum of competitor revenues, total end-user spend |
| **Industry Penetration** | % of TAM currently addressed = total industry revenue / TAM | Sum of all competitors' revenue / TAM |
| **Company Market Share** | Company revenue / total industry revenue | % — tracked quarterly |
| **Price / Take Rate** | Average selling price as % of customer budget; platform take rate | ASP trend, take rate %, pricing power index |
| **Product Mix** | Revenue shift toward higher/lower-value products | Revenue per unit, attach rate, premium product % of revenue |

### Share Gain Source Analysis

| Is share gain from… | Signal | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Taking competitors?** | Competitor revenue declining or flat; win/loss data showing direct competitive displacements | Zero-sum: must maintain competitive edge; risk of retaliation |
| **Market expansion?** | Category growing faster than company revenue; new customer segments entering; low penetration ratio | Positive-sum: more room to run; less competitive friction |
| **Both?** | Company share up AND category penetration rising | Best case — category tailwinds + competitive execution |

### Share Loss Risk Factors

- Price war initiated by a well-capitalised competitor
- Technology discontinuity that renders current product obsolete
- Key customer concentration (top 3 customers > 30% of revenue)
- Channel conflict or partner defection
- Regulatory change that advantages a new entrant
- Substitute technology reaching feature parity at lower cost

| Competitor | Advantage | Weakness | Threat Level | Monitoring Signal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Competitor A** (public) | [Technology / Brand / Scale / Channel] | [Cost structure / Product gap / Geographic hole] | High / Med / Low | [Pricing change, key customer win/loss, product launch, hiring shift] |
| **Competitor B** (public) | [Network effects / Regulatory moat / Data advantage] | [Innovation velocity / Margin pressure / Talent retention] | High / Med / Low | [Quarterly results, R&D headcount change, M&A activity] |
| **Competitor C** (private / VC-backed) | [Speed of iteration / Aggressive pricing / Founder-led sales] | [Balance sheet / Unit economics / Brand awareness] | High / Med / Low | [Funding round, valuation, hiring from key roles, customer win announcements] |
| **Customer in-sourcing** | [Full control over roadmap / No margin paid to vendor / Data privacy] | [Scale economics / Innovation velocity / Talent concentration] | High / Med / Low | [Hiring for internal build teams, RFPs declining, open-source adoption] |
| **Substitute technology** | [Lower price point / Differentiated approach / Open-source ecosystem] | [Feature gaps / Ecosystem immaturity / Switching costs] | Med / Low | [Developer community growth, integration with adjacent tools, enterprise case studies] |

### Competitive Threat Assessment Questions

| # | Question | Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Are competitors cutting prices? | Price war risk; commoditisation of category |
| 2 | Are competitors expanding capacity? | Supply increase → future price pressure; signals confidence in demand |
| 3 | Are competitors winning key customers? | Direct share loss; check win/loss database for root cause |
| 4 | Are competitors ahead on technology? | Feature gap analysis; patent filing trends; R&D spend trajectory |
| 5 | Could customers in-source the product? | If switching costs are low and build-vs-buy math shifts, risk is real |
| 6 | Is substitute technology viable? | Feature parity timeline; adoption curve; total cost of ownership comparison |
| 7 | Are competitors hiring aggressively from the company? | Talent flight risk; check LinkedIn hires from company in last 6 months |
| 8 | Are competitors forming exclusive partnerships? | Channel lock-out risk; check for exclusive distribution or co-sell agreements |
